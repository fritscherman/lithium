Inside a Lithium-Ion Cell

  • Anode (Negative)

    Typically made of graphite. During charging, lithium ions intercalate between graphite layers, storing energy. On discharge they flow back out.

  • Cathode (Positive)

    Usually a lithium metal oxide (e.g. LiCoO₂, LFP, NMC). It provides the lithium ions and determines energy density and voltage.

  • Electrolyte

    A lithium salt dissolved in an organic solvent (e.g. LiPF₆ in EC/DMC). It conducts Li⁺ ions between electrodes but blocks electrons.

  • Separator

    A thin porous membrane (polyethylene or polypropylene) that keeps anode and cathode apart to prevent short-circuits while allowing ion flow.

  • Casing

    A steel or aluminium shell (cylindrical, prismatic, or pouch) that encloses all components and connects to external terminals.

How Does It Work?

🔌

Charging

An external voltage forces electrons through the circuit. Li⁺ ions leave the cathode, cross the electrolyte, and insert into graphite layers of the anode.

⚡

Discharging

When powering a device, Li⁺ ions spontaneously move back to the cathode through the electrolyte. Electrons travel through the external circuit — that's the current you use.

🔄

Intercalation

Lithium ions slide between atomic layers of electrode materials without permanently changing their structure — enabling hundreds to thousands of charge cycles.

🛡️

SEI Layer

A thin Solid Electrolyte Interphase forms on the anode during first cycles. It stabilises the electrode and protects electrolyte from further decomposition.

Pros & Cons

Advantages

  • High energy density — lightweight & compact
  • Low self-discharge rate
  • No memory effect
  • High charge/discharge efficiency
  • Long cycle life with modern chemistries
  • Wide temperature range
  • Scalable from mAh to MWh

Limitations

  • Capacity degrades over time
  • Risk of thermal runaway if damaged
  • Requires a Battery Management System
  • Performance drops in extreme cold
  • Contains scarce materials (cobalt, lithium)
  • Recycling infrastructure still maturing
  • Higher upfront cost than lead-acid

A Brief History

1970s
Stanley Whittingham — First Li Battery
Whittingham at Exxon demonstrated the first rechargeable lithium cell using a TiS₂ cathode and lithium metal anode.
1980
John Goodenough — LiCoO₂ Cathode
Goodenough discovered that LiCoO₂ could serve as a high-voltage cathode, unlocking the modern Li-ion chemistry.
1985
Akira Yoshino — Graphite Anode
Yoshino replaced the dangerous lithium metal anode with safer carbonaceous material (graphite), enabling commercialisation.
1991
Sony — First Commercial Li-ion Cell
Sony shipped the first commercial Li-ion batteries for camcorders, revolutionising portable electronics.
2000s
Smartphones & Laptops
Lithium-ion became the de-facto standard for consumer electronics, driving massive investment and cost reductions.
2010s
Electric Vehicles & Grid Storage
Tesla and others proved Li-ion could power cars. Grid-scale battery storage projects appeared globally.
2019
Nobel Prize in Chemistry
Whittingham, Goodenough & Yoshino shared the Nobel Prize for developing lithium-ion batteries.
2020s+
Solid-State & Next-Gen
Solid-state electrolytes, sodium-ion alternatives, and silicon anodes promise even higher energy density and safety.
